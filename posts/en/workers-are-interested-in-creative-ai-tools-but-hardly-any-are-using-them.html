High Worker Interest in Creative AI Tools Contrasts with Low Workplace Adoption

www.fastcompany.com · 2026-07-22T21:00:00

Although ninety percent of surveyed workers desire to use creative artificial intelligence, only nine percent currently implement these tools due to training gaps and organizational hurdles.

A recent Adobe survey reveals a significant disconnect between employee enthusiasm for creative artificial intelligence and its actual implementation in the workplace. While 90% of full-time U.S. workers surveyed expressed an interest in utilizing creative AI tools—such as image generation, graphic design, and brainstorming—only 9% report regularly incorporating them into their daily workflows. Instead, employees primarily rely on AI for routine technical tasks, including document summarization, data analysis, and topic research, despite 72% believing creative AI could enhance their overall workplace creativity.

Several key obstacles contribute to this disparity between interest and practical execution. Nearly half of the respondents indicated uncertainty regarding how creative AI applies to their specific job roles. Furthermore, employees cited structural limitations, including a lack of employer-provided training, unclear corporate policies, high software costs, and integration challenges. On average, workers report spending 7.5 hours weekly on tasks that could benefit from creative AI, highlighting a substantial untapped potential for productivity and innovation if these organizational hurdles are addressed.

Broader workplace trends mirrored in Gallup research confirm that while general AI adoption is rising across sectors like writing and research, specialized creative generation remains underutilized. To bridge this gap, experts and survey respondents suggest that companies offer targeted training, clear usage guidelines, and robust data privacy assurances. However, organizations integrating these tools must balance potential creativity gains against the risk of overreliance, ensuring that AI complements rather than replaces human critical thinking and genuine innovation.
