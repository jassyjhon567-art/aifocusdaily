Developer Uses $5 Dongle to Block Over 500,000 Ad Domains in Tiny Flash Memory

www.techradar.com · Wed, 22 Jul 2026 23:20:00 +0000

Egyptian developer ZedAxis created a $5 ad-blocking microcontroller dongle that compresses 537,000 domain names into 4MB of flash memory for efficient network-level filtering.

Egyptian software developer ZedAxis has unveiled an innovative hardware-based solution designed to effectively combat intrusive online advertisements across local networks. By utilizing a low-cost microcontroller dongle priced at just five dollars, the developer successfully managed to squeeze a massive database containing 537,000 ad-serving domains into a mere four megabytes of onboard flash memory. This impressive technical feat demonstrates an exceptionally efficient approach to network-wide ad blocking, providing everyday users with a lightweight alternative to resource-intensive browser extensions or costly dedicated server setups.

In a detailed video demonstration published on YouTube, ZedAxis showcased the custom hardware actively filtering web traffic in real time. To fit over half a million domain addresses into such a severely constrained memory space, the project relies on sophisticated data compression algorithms and memory optimization techniques. These methods enable the tiny microcontroller to rapidly cross-reference incoming web requests against its internal blocklist, successfully intercepting and dropping ad-related connections before the unwanted content can ever load on connected devices.

The project highlights the tremendous potential of ultra-affordable microcontrollers in personal cybersecurity, data saving, and privacy-enhancing applications. As modern online advertising becomes increasingly pervasive, bandwidth-heavy, and tracking-intensive, DIY hardware filters offer an accessible way for consumers to take control of their network traffic. By proving that robust network-level ad blocking can operate on a device costing less than a simple meal, ZedAxis demonstrates how clever software engineering can make digital privacy practical and affordable for a much wider audience.
