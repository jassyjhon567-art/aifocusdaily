Deep-Sea Snails Travel Ocean Surface Currents to Colonize Remote Hydrothermal Vents

www.sciencenews.org · Tue, 21 Jul 2026 17:30:00 +0000

Scientists have discovered that deep-sea snail larvae ride surface ocean currents to travel vast distances and colonize isolated hydrothermal vents, revealing strong links between surface and abyssal ecosystems.

Scientists have discovered that tiny larvae of deep-sea snails utilize ocean surface currents to traverse vast distances between isolated hydrothermal vent ecosystems. Hydrothermal vents are extreme environments located thousands of meters below the sea surface, where specialized organisms thrive in total darkness. Previously, researchers struggled to explain how species colonize far-flung vent systems separated by thousands of kilometers of barren ocean floor.

The study reveals that larval snails ascend from the extreme depths of the ocean to upper ocean currents, where powerful surface waters transport them across expansive geographical gaps. Once these larvae reach suitable locations near distant hydrothermal vents, they descend back into the abyss to settle and mature into adulthood. This remarkable journey highlights a dynamic biological bridge connecting the ocean's sunlit surface layer with its deepest, most isolated benthic habitats.

These findings significantly alter scientists' understanding of marine connectivity and ecosystem resilience in the deep ocean. By establishing that deep-sea life relies on surface currents for genetic dispersal and colonization, the research emphasizes the intricate link between surface oceanic processes and extreme abyssal environments. Consequently, environmental changes affecting upper ocean currents could have unintended downstream effects on distant hydrothermal vent communities that were long thought to be entirely self-contained.
