End-to-End Encryption Sparks New Conflicts in the Ongoing 'Going Dark' Debate

www.schneier.com · 2026-07-23T11:03:50Z

A new research paper examines the evolving 'Going Dark' debate, analyzing how current end-to-end encryption controversies differ from earlier historical phases of cryptographic regulation.

A new legal and policy research paper reexamines the global debate surrounding encryption and law enforcement access, marking what scholars call the "Third Round" of the contentious "Going Dark" controversy. As governments worldwide increasingly propose and implement legal frameworks to restrict or bypass end-to-end encryption (E2EE) in the name of national security and crime prevention, researchers are analyzing the technological and market shifts driving these policy updates. The paper aims to provide policymakers and legal experts with a clearer understanding of modern cryptographic mechanisms and their broad societal implications.

To contextualize current developments, the authors trace the historical evolution of encryption policy across three distinct eras. The first round, known as the "Crypto Wars" of the 1990s, focused primarily on United States export controls over strong cryptographic software, which eventually collapsed in 1999. The second round, spanning roughly from 2010 to 2015, saw widespread adoption of encryption-in-transit across the internet. However, because cloud service providers retained access to unencrypted data stored on their servers, law enforcement enjoyed unprecedented access to digital evidence, creating what legal experts termed a "golden age of surveillance."

The current third round centers on end-to-end encryption, an architecture where only the communicating users hold the decryption keys, rendering data unreadable to service providers, telecommunication companies, and governments alike. While privacy advocates champion E2EE as essential for safeguarding digital rights and cybersecurity, law enforcement agencies argue it severely hinders criminal investigations. The research paper emphasizes the urgent need for informed policy discussions that critically evaluate legislative proposals against technological realities, seeking a balanced approach between individual privacy and public safety.
